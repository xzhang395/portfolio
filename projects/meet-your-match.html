Meet Your Match

April 2018, ~12 hours


How can we make help an adopter find a pet which matches their lifestyle?

RESEARCH

To understand the current adoption process and experience, I visited an animal shelter in Pittsburgh, interviewed and observed 3 shelter staff, 3 adopters and 1 potential adopter.

People blindly choose the cute ones without thinking about compatibility and responsibility

Young and cute animals are the most popular. Animal returns happen once in a while because of incompatibility. Major adoption websites have filters only for physical characteristics like breed, age or gender instead of behavior or personalities.


“Many seniors don't understand that they can't handle a puppy.

The selection is mutual

It is every shelter staff's goal to find the best home for all animals. Many shelters interview potential adopters for their qualification.

“Our animals are our clients.

“ We don't like college students as adopters because they return the animals after moving away.

People with clear preference have trouble finding the animal meets their requirement

For adaptors who are fully prepared, finding the exact type of animal they want help build long-lasting relationships. However, the availability of the animal changes frequently while information online rarely gets updated.

“I called about the cat I saw online, they told me it's already adopted.

DESIGN CRITERIA

Reframed the prompt after synthetizing the research insights to guide design direction:

How might we help adopters make informed and responsible decisions?

A successful design should:

  • Help targeted adopter to find exactly what they need.
  • Help exploratory adopters understand their constrains.
  • Educate impulsive adopters to make responsive decisions.
  • Alleviate the burden of shelter workers to inform adopters.

ITERATIONS

sketches and paper prototypes

After some quick sketches, I narrowed down to 5 potential ideas to gather user feedback.


Adoption Profile
Fill out a mandatory survey to receive a curated list of matching each day.


Foster Before Adoption
Recommend the hesitant or inexperienced adopters to foster first in order to understand the real living conditions with their new pet.


Adoption Event Finder
To leverage the emotion factor when adopters physically interact with their potential pet.


Crawling Social Media
Allow people to get newest updates on the shelter animals and use social media as an entry point.


Adoption Chatbot
Instead of navigating through an interface, ask or answer questions from the Chatbot to get recommendations and custom experience.

Main takeaways after testing with 3 users:

  • People appreciate learning more about themselves before adoption.
  • Like having a curated list to make decision-making easier but still want to see all the options.
  • Like the idea of having a foster period before adoption, yet concerned that constant moving can traumatize the animals.
  • Don't like using social media for adoption because it only shows the bright side.

Refined direction: Finding a scientific way of matching - ASPCA’s Meet Your Match

The ASPCA's Meet Your Match is a research-based adoptions program designed to increase the likelihood that shelter dogs and cats will bond with their adopters. Shelters who use the Meet Your Match adoption program experienced significant improvements in the numbers of adoptions and returns.

DESIGN DECISIONS

Onboarding: Build trust in the matching algorithm

A. The early iteration has only one screen explaining the matching algorithm. During testing, people expressed confusion and distrust in the matching process.

The final design devotes three screens explaining the research behind the matching algorithm. Encouraging people to trust the matching results.

Browsing: Discourage judgement from a single profile picture, read more and get to know every match

A. When the user starts browsing the matching results. Rather than having a shopping-like experience, I want them to slow down and read the information about each match in more details. As a result, full screen cards scrolling sideways is chosen rather than an infinite scroll of smaller cards.

B. However, one problem with having big cards scrolling sideway is navigating back and forth to find certain information. To solve the problem, a scrollable row of profile pictures are positioned on the top for fast navigation.

D. An auto-played video is used to bring the animal to life and give a sneak peak into the animal's personality.

E. Use first-person narrative to simulate conversation and build connection. The amount of information provided is tested to be just enough.

Filtering: Discourage the use of filter. Yet, help adopters with strong preference find their dream companion with a "wishlist".

A. Encouraging adopters to open up to more possibilities, the filter is designed to take less visual attention.

B. Yet it is understandable that people have preferences. Filter is provided for adopters who have strong preference on breed, age or gender. It is likely that none of the shelter has the exact match at the moment. User can choose to add their preference to watch and receive an email notification when a new match is found.

Fostering: Not fully prepared for adoption or disagree with the matching recommendation? Try foster before adoption.
